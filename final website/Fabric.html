How to choose Fabric

Choosing a fabric is one of the main challenges in first starting a sewing project. Each fabric has different properties ranging from the delicacy of the fabric of the material it is made from. For instance, working with silk requires the Sewist to be gentle with the fabric to cause no damage or stains. Silk is a much more delicate type of fabric as opposed to polyester which is much more resistant to wear and tear.

At first, it can be hard trying to determine the type of fabric you may be working with, especially if the tag has been removed but, over time with enough practice, you will be able to tell what fabric you are using based on the texture alone. Here beside this paragraph is the most common types of fabric used, a description of how to identify them, and how you can use them for sewing projects.

Name Description Purpose
Linen Linen fabrics are heavier than their cotton counterparts, and they are approximately 30% stronger. They often feel more crisp initially, though through time and use, linen fabrics become soft and supple to the touch. aprons, bags, towels (swimming, bath, beach, body and wash towels), napkins, bed linens, tablecloths, runners, chair covers, and men's and women's wear.
Cotton Cotton is a natural hollow fibers; they are soft, cool, known as breathable fibers and absorbent. Cotton fibers can hold water 24–27 times their own weight. They are strong, dye absorbent and can stand up against abrasion wear and high temperature. cotton can be used to make practically any type of textile for consumer or industrial use.
Silk Silk is the strongest natural textile in the world. silk produces a shimmering optical effect, which is due to the triangular prism-like structure of silk fibers. These prisms reflect light at various angles, which results in the subtle rainbow hue The most common consumer application of silk is apparel production
Nylon Nylon is the name of a family of synthetic polymers that are commonly used to make a variety of different types of apparel and consumer goods. Unlike other organic or semi-synthetic fibers, nylon fibers are entirely synthetic, which means that they have no basis in organic material. Tights, stockings, sportswear, yoga pants, and other form-fitting types of apparel
Wool Wool is a type of fabric derived from the hairs of various animals. Wool is known for its durability and thermally insulating properties; depending on the type of hair that producers use to make wool, this fabric may benefit from the natural insulative effects that keep the animal that produced the hair warm throughout the winter. sweaters, hats, gloves, and other types of accessories and apparel.
Polyester Polyester is one of the world’s most popular textiles, and it is used in thousands of different consumer and industrial applications. polyester may be the sole constituent of apparel products, but it’s more common for polyester to be blended with cotton or another natural fiber. Shirts, pants, hoodies, dresses, jackets, underwear, socks, blankets, hats, sheets, rope, upholstery
Felt Felt is a type of matted fabric that consists of textile fibers condensed and pressed together. Traditionally made with wool or another type of animal fur, it’s now possible to make felt with acrylic and other forms of synthetic fibers. Hats, insulating garment lining, boot liners, arts and crafts, pillows, bags, details on other garments
Lace Lace is a delicate, weblike fabric that textile producers can manufacture using a wide range of techniques. Varying significantly in complexity, different types of lace are commonly used to accentuate or decorate garments, upholstery, and homewares, and this fabric is only rarely used to make entire textile products. Bridal gowns, shawls, dresses, garment accentuations, accessory accentuations, upholstery accentuations, curtains, tablecloths, lampshades, lingerie, scarves
Leather Leather remains, however, one of the most desirable textile products due to its durability, water-resistance, insulative properties, and luxurious softness. Available in many different styles, grades, and colors, leather is one of the most diversified natural textiles. Jackets, coats, wallets, belts, bags, gloves, shoes, book bindings, upholstery, automobile seats, saddles, luggage, sporting goods
